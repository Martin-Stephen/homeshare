03 · The Liquidity Spectrum
Honestly, where Homeshare sits · and what a syndicator actually gets

Illiquid property doesn't become liquid because you add a secondary market. It becomes better-managed.

Liquidity is a spectrum. Every public failure in the NZ property-syndicate category sits at one end — locked in until sponsor resolution, with reputational damage attaching to the manager. Daily-NAV managed funds sit at the other — but they can only exist on top of genuinely liquid underlyings. Homeshare is deliberately in the middle, with a design that manages illiquidity rather than pretending to eliminate it.

When an investor can't exit, they blame the manager. Every public case teaches retail and advisers to avoid locked-in syndicates — that's a category-level brand tax, not a Homeshare sales hook.

Where Homeshare sits on the spectrum — and what it actually buys the investor
MechanismWhat it does · what it doesn't
Investor-to-investor secondary±25% AVM band (cl 12)Does: provides an on-platform venue for willing buyers and sellers to match without waiting for sponsor wind-up. Doesn't: guarantee a match. A sell order can sit in the book if no buyer turns up — particularly early-AUM or during correlated-sell events.
AVM price disciplineCoreLogic · cl 7(j)(1)Does: prevents panic-floor collapse by capping the price corridor at ±25% of an independent valuation refreshed every 4 months. Doesn't: help if every holder wants to sell at once — floor is still the floor.
Cross-sub-trust diversificationSame deed, multiple dealsDoes: lets an investor hold units across 5–10 properties; their exit demand isn't correlated to any single asset's performance. Doesn't: exist at pre-revenue — depth comes from acquirer distribution and multi-property scale.
Manager discretionSecondary Market Rules, cl 12Does: lets the acquirer set matching logic, introduce transaction fees, warehouse stranded orders, or add a balance-sheet-backed market-maker if they choose. Doesn't: force any of that — these are levers the acquirer can pull, not promises we've made.
Non-redeemable by designDeed cl 12.11Does: prevents Oyster-style "redemptions suspended" headline — you can't suspend what doesn't exist. Doesn't: give investors a fund-level exit — the only exit is the secondary market (or eventual wind-up).
Honest positioning: one rung up from locked-in, several rungs below daily-NAV liquid. That rung matters — it's where investor confidence, regulatory acceptance, and brand control all live. But it's one rung, not salvation.

What the acquirer gets from this rung. (i) Brand insulation from "suspended redemptions" coverage — the structure makes that headline impossible. (ii) An orderly exit venue investors can plan around. (iii) Price discipline via AVM when markets are thin. (iv) Operating levers (cl 12) to tune depth over time. (v) A structurally honest product to take to advisers and retail. What the acquirer does NOT get: a liquidity guarantee, a daily-NAV wrapper, or a promise that every sell order matches. Real depth requires real scale — the acquirer's distribution produces it; Homeshare's infrastructure enables it. Full depth disclosure on slide 10B.
